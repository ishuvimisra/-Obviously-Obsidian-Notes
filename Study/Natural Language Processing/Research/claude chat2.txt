This is your bias mitigation training pipeline for the Jigsaw toxicity project. Let me break down the why behind each architectural decision.

The Core Problem Being Solved
Standard DistilBERT trained on Jigsaw data learns a shortcut: comments mentioning identities like "muslim", "gay", "black" get flagged as toxic more often — not because they are toxic, but because the dataset is biased. Your pipeline fights this by penalizing the model more harshly when it makes mistakes on identity-containing comments.

1. BiasMitigationTrainer — The Custom Loss Function
pythonloss_fct = nn.CrossEntropyLoss(reduction='none')  # per-sample loss, not averaged yet
loss = loss_fct(logits, labels)
loss = (loss * weights).mean()                    # identity comments get weight=3.0, others=1.0
```

The key insight is `reduction='none'`. Normally CrossEntropyLoss collapses all sample errors into one number immediately. By deferring that, you get a **vector of per-sample losses** — which you can then *scale* before averaging.

A comment with `weight=3.0` contributes 3× more to the gradient update. The model gets "punished harder" for misclassifying identity mentions, forcing it to learn their actual toxicity signal rather than demographic correlation.

---

### 2. `compute_metrics` — Why Not Just Accuracy?

On an imbalanced dataset (~90% non-toxic), a model that predicts "not toxic" for everything gets **90% accuracy** — useless. F1 is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, so it only scores high if both are high. This is why F1 is the real signal here.

---

### 3. `TrainingArguments` — The Critical Flags

| Flag | Why It Matters |
|------|----------------|
| `remove_unused_columns=False` | HuggingFace auto-drops columns not in the model's `forward()` signature. Your `weights` column isn't a standard BERT input — without this flag, it gets silently deleted before `compute_loss` ever sees it. Your entire bias mitigation scheme breaks invisibly. |
| `fp16=True` | Stores activations in 16-bit floats. Halves memory, ~2× speed on T4. Minor precision tradeoff, acceptable for fine-tuning. |
| `warmup_steps=500` | Starts with a very small learning rate, ramps up. Prevents early large gradient updates from destroying the pretrained weights. |
| `weight_decay=0.01` | L2 regularization — penalizes large weights, reduces overfitting. |

---

### The Data Flow (Mental Model)
```
Raw text
  → Tokenizer → input_ids, attention_mask
  → You manually add: weights (1.0 or 3.0 per sample)
  → Trainer calls compute_loss()
  → weights column is *popped* from inputs before model sees it
  → model sees only: input_ids, attention_mask (standard BERT inputs)
  → per-sample loss × weights → biased gradient signal
  → model parameters update, learning to not conflate identity with toxicity

What Problem Are We Actually Solving?
Imagine you're a content moderator at a large news platform. Your job is to flag toxic comments before they go public. You have millions of comments per day — too many for humans to read. So you build an AI to do it automatically.
The AI reads a comment and answers one question: is this toxic or not?
Seems straightforward. But here's what actually happens when you train such an AI on real-world data: it learns shortcuts. Instead of understanding why a comment is harmful, it notices that certain words appear more often in toxic comments. Words like "muslim", "black", "gay", "women". Not because those groups are toxic — but because the dataset reflects a world where those groups are talked about more often in toxic contexts.
The AI internalises this as a rule: identity mention → probably toxic.
Now your moderation system is doing something quietly harmful. A perfectly civil discussion about Muslim immigration policy gets flagged. A comment defending Black Lives Matter gets removed. Meanwhile, a subtly racist comment that avoids identity words slips through.
This is demographic bias in toxicity classification. That is what this entire project is about — measuring it, understanding it, and fixing it.

The Dataset: Jigsaw Unintended Bias in Toxicity Classification
The data comes from a Kaggle competition run by Google's Jigsaw team. It contains ~1.8 million real comments from the Civil Comments platform — a now-defunct comment hosting service used by news sites.
Each comment has two important things:

A toxicity score (0 to 1, given by human raters)
Identity scores — how much does this comment mention specific demographic groups (male, female, muslim, christian, black, white, gay/lesbian, jewish)?

The identity scores are the key. They let us ask: does the model behave differently on comments that mention specific groups? That's the entire fairness question.

The Phases: A Journey From Naive to Rigorous
Think of the project as five increasingly sophisticated attempts to answer one question: can we build a toxicity classifier that is accurate AND fair?

Phase 1 — The Naive Baseline
What was built: A simple, fast classifier. Text is converted to a bag of word frequencies (TF-IDF), fed into Logistic Regression.
What it achieved: 94.6% accuracy. Looks impressive.
What it was hiding: The dataset is 92% non-toxic. A model that calls everything non-toxic gets 92% accuracy. The actual toxic recall was only 30% — the model was missing 70% of actual toxic comments. The high accuracy number was a lie told by class imbalance.
The fairness audit revealed: The model had meaningful bias gaps. The muslim group had an FPR gap of 0.055 and FNR gap of 0.125 — both "Serious" by our threshold. The model was simultaneously over-flagging innocent Muslim-mentioning comments AND missing actual toxicity targeting Muslims.
Intuition: This model is like a security guard who flags everyone wearing a turban but waves through politely-worded threatening letters.

Phase 2 — Measuring the Bias Formally
What was built: Not a new model — a measurement framework.
The key metrics:

FPR Gap (False Positive Rate Gap): How much more often does the model wrongly flag a clean comment mentioning Group X compared to the average? This is over-flagging — treating innocent identity mentions as suspicious.
FNR Gap (False Negative Rate Gap): How much more often does the model miss actual toxicity when Group X is mentioned? This is under-protection — failing to catch hate targeting that group.

An ideal fair model has both gaps at zero. Our LR baseline had serious gaps on multiple groups.
Why this matters: These two errors have different real-world consequences. A high FPR means the platform silences legitimate voices — Muslim commenters, Black commenters — who get falsely flagged. A high FNR means the platform fails to protect those same groups from actual hate speech. Both failures harm the same communities, in different ways.

Phase 3 — First Mitigation Attempt (Flat Weighting)
What was built: DistilBERT — a compressed transformer model — fine-tuned on the Jigsaw data with a custom loss function. Identity-mentioning comments were given 3× more weight in training.
The logic: If the model gets penalised more for mistakes on identity comments, it should pay more attention to getting those right.
What it achieved: 83% accuracy on a balanced test set. Better contextual understanding than LR.
The problem left unsolved: The 3× weight was arbitrary. No mathematical justification. And critically — no vanilla BERT was trained as a control. Without a control, there's no way to know if the weighting helped at all. This is the key scientific gap Phase 3 left open.

Phase 4 — The Controlled Experiment
What was built: Three models evaluated on the exact same test set:

LR Baseline (loaded from Phase 1)
Vanilla BERT (no bias intervention — the control)
Adaptive BERT (weights derived from Phase 2 FPR gaps — data-driven, not arbitrary)

The surprising finding: Both BERT models were dramatically worse than LR on FPR gaps. For black, Vanilla BERT's FPR gap was 0.78 — meaning comments mentioning Black people were flagged as toxic at a rate 78 percentage points higher than other comments. LR's gap was 0.25, bad but far less catastrophic.
Why did this happen? BERT's power is contextual understanding. It reads the whole sentence. But that power cut both ways — it learned to associate identity terms with toxicity more aggressively than LR, because it had seen more patterns linking them. The adaptive weighting made this worse by telling the model "pay extra attention when identity words appear" — which reinforced the spurious correlation.
The silver lining: FNR gaps improved. BERT does catch more actual toxicity targeting identity groups. The problem is asymmetric — better at finding real hate, but terrible at ignoring innocent mentions.
The Phase 4 insight that drives Phase 5: You cannot use the same mechanism (upweighting) to solve two opposite errors. FPR and FNR need different interventions.

Phase 5 — Three Simultaneous Fixes
This is where the project currently is. Three techniques deployed together:
Fix 1 — Asymmetric Loss Weights
Instead of treating all identity comments the same, we now split by label:

Toxic comment mentioning an identity → weight 3.0× (catch this, it's real harm)
Clean comment mentioning an identity → weight 0.4× (be conservative, don't over-flag)

The 0.4× downweight on clean identity comments directly tells the gradient: "stop treating identity mentions as evidence of toxicity." This is the targeted FPR fix.
Fix 2 — HateBERT Backbone
DistilBERT was trained on Wikipedia and books — clean, formal, encyclopaedic text. It has never seen how people actually write hate speech online: coded language, dog whistles, plausible deniability phrasing, indirect attacks.
HateBERT was trained on 1.5 million posts from Reddit communities that were banned for hate speech. It understands the register of online toxicity. This directly addresses the FNR problem — comments the model missed in Phase 4 (genuine toxicity with confidence 0.02) are exactly the kind of indirect, structurally subtle hostility that HateBERT was built to recognise.
Fix 3 — Counterfactual Data Augmentation (CDA)
For every training comment mentioning "muslim", we generate a twin with "christian" substituted, keeping the same label. For every "black" comment, a "white" twin. Same toxicity label, different identity word.
The model now sees that the same sentence structure with different identity terms has the same toxicity classification. This directly breaks the demographic co-occurrence shortcut. The model can no longer use "mentions muslim" as evidence for toxicity — it has seen equally toxic and non-toxic sentences about every group.
Cross-Dataset Evaluation on HateXplain
After training on Jigsaw, we test on HateXplain — a completely different dataset, different source (Twitter/Gab), different annotators, different annotation schema.
This answers the most important scientific question of the whole project: did we train a model that genuinely understands toxicity, or did we train a model that fits Jigsaw's specific annotation biases?
If performance collapses on HateXplain, that is the demographic drift finding — the model learned Jigsaw-specific patterns, not universal harm. That's not a failure of the project. That is the paper's central empirical contribution.

The World This Project Lives In
Every major online platform — news sites, social media, comment sections — has a content moderation problem. There are billions of comments posted daily. Human moderators cannot read all of them. So platforms build automated systems: AI models that read a comment and decide whether to flag it as toxic.
These systems are now infrastructure. They determine whose speech gets amplified and whose gets silenced. They run invisibly, at scale, with real consequences for real people.
The problem is that these systems are broken in a specific, quiet, systematic way — and most people building them don't notice, because the standard way of measuring performance (accuracy) completely hides the problem.

The Problem: What Accuracy Doesn't Tell You
Imagine you trained a toxicity detector on a dataset of one million comments. 92% of them are non-toxic, 8% are toxic. You train a model and it gets 93% accuracy. Impressive.
Now ask: what does 93% accuracy actually mean here?
It means the model can predict the majority class (non-toxic) most of the time and still score well. In fact, a model that calls every single comment non-toxic — without reading any of them — would score 92% accuracy. Your model is barely better than that.
But the deeper problem isn't even the class imbalance. It's what the model learned to do on the 8% of toxic comments. Suppose it catches 90% of toxic comments about politicians but only 30% of toxic comments targeting Black people. Or suppose it flags 40% of innocent comments mentioning Muslims as toxic. The aggregate accuracy number tells you nothing about this. You'd publish it as a success.
This is the gap that motivated the entire field of algorithmic fairness in NLP. And this is precisely the problem your project addresses.

The Research Gap: What Existed and What Was Missing
What existed before your project
The dataset: Google's Jigsaw team released the Civil Comments dataset in 2019 with a landmark paper by Borkan et al. They noticed that toxicity models were exhibiting what they called "unintended bias" — the model's toxicity predictions correlated with which demographic groups were mentioned in a comment, independent of whether the comment was actually harmful. A comment saying "I am a gay man" would be flagged as toxic simply because the word "gay" appeared. They proposed new metrics (subgroup AUC, background positive subgroup negative AUC) to measure this.
What Borkan et al. did not solve: They defined and measured the problem. They didn't systematically test which training-time interventions actually reduce it, or whether reducing one type of error (over-flagging) causes another type (missing real hate) to get worse.
The loss-weighting idea: Dixon et al. (2018), also at Google/Jigsaw, proposed a straightforward fix: if identity-mentioning comments are disproportionately misclassified, upweight them during training. Give the model a bigger penalty for getting these wrong. This intuition is sound but their paper tested it only in terms of accuracy improvement — they did not separately measure FPR and FNR gaps, so they couldn't see whether upweighting helped both error types or only one.
Counterfactual Data Augmentation (CDA): Lu et al. (2020) and Hall Maudslay et al. (2019) proposed generating "counterfactual" training pairs — for every sentence mentioning "he", create a twin mentioning "she" with the same label. This teaches the model that gender itself is not a signal. They demonstrated this for gender bias in coreference resolution. It had not been systematically applied to the joint FPR+FNR problem in toxicity classification.
HateBERT: Caselli et al. (2021) showed that domain-specific pretraining on actual hate speech data (Reddit banned communities) produces better representations of toxic language than general BERT. But they evaluated only on hate speech detection accuracy — not on demographic fairness gaps.
What nobody had cleanly done: Take all three interventions (asymmetric loss weighting, CDA, domain-specific backbone), combine them in a controlled experiment where each model is evaluated on the same test set, measure FPR gap and FNR gap separately for each demographic group, then test generalisation on an out-of-domain dataset. That's your project.
The research gap your project fills
The gap is specifically this: the interaction between FPR mitigation and FNR mitigation under loss-weighting approaches had not been systematically characterised. Prior work treated "reducing bias" as a single objective. Your Phase 4 finding — that symmetric upweighting dramatically worsens FPR while improving FNR — is a concrete empirical demonstration that these are two distinct, partially anti-correlated objectives that require separate mechanisms to address simultaneously.
The secondary gap: the asymmetric loss approach for toxicity fairness. Assigning downweights specifically to clean identity-mentioning comments (not just upweights to all identity comments) had not been tested in this exact form on this dataset with this measurement framework.

Phase by Phase: Intuition and Why
Phase 1 — Why Start with Logistic Regression?
The naive question: Why bother with a simple model at all when BERT exists?
The answer: You need a baseline. In any scientific experiment, you need a control condition — something to compare against. Without a baseline, you cannot claim improvement. If BERT scores 83% and you have no reference point, you don't know if that's good or bad.
But there's a deeper reason. The Logistic Regression baseline tells you something important: how much of the bias problem is inherent in the dataset itself, before any sophisticated modelling. If LR already shows strong FPR gaps for muslim, that means the annotation pattern in the Jigsaw data is itself biased — human annotators were more likely to label Muslim-mentioning comments as toxic, regardless of content. You can see this in your Phase 2 results: LR had relatively small FPR gaps (0.0079 for muslim) compared to BERT (0.44). This contrast is significant — it means something about how BERT learns made the problem worse, not just that the dataset is biased.
What TF-IDF does: It converts text into a vector of word frequencies. Each comment becomes a list of numbers: how many times did each word appear? The Logistic Regression then learns a weight for each word — positive weight means this word pushes toward toxic, negative weight means it pushes toward clean.
Why this causes bias: If the word "muslim" appears 1000 times in toxic comments and 200 times in clean comments in the training data, LR will learn a positive weight for "muslim." Not because the word is harmful, but because the training data has that distribution. This is the spurious correlation problem — the model learns a correlation that happens to exist in the data rather than the causal relationship (sentiment + intent = toxicity).
The critical discovery in Phase 2: When you ran the fairness audit on LR predictions, you found something counterintuitive. LR's FPR gaps were actually small for most groups — especially muslim (0.0079) and christian (0.0080). Its FNR gaps were larger (muslim 0.0303, male 0.0788). This becomes important context for interpreting Phase 4.

Phase 2 — Why FPR and FNR? Why Not Just Accuracy?
The intuition problem with accuracy:
Suppose you have 100 comments mentioning "muslim" in your test set. 10 are actually toxic, 90 are clean. Your model flags 30 as toxic. Of those 30, 8 are genuinely toxic and 22 are clean comments that got wrongly flagged.
Now do the same for 100 comments that don't mention any identity. 10 are toxic, 90 are clean. Your model flags 12 as toxic. Of those 12, 9 are genuinely toxic and 3 are clean comments wrongly flagged.
Accuracy on both groups might look similar. But the model is treating Muslim-mentioning comments very differently: it's flagging innocent ones at a much higher rate (22/90 = 24%) versus non-identity comments (3/90 = 3%).
The False Positive Rate (FPR) captures this exactly: of all the truly clean comments in a group, what fraction does the model wrongly flag as toxic? A high FPR gap means the model is systematically more suspicious of one group's innocent speech.
The False Negative Rate (FNR) captures the opposite harm: of all the truly toxic comments targeting a group, what fraction does the model miss? A high FNR gap means the model is worse at protecting a group from actual hate speech.
Both are real, distinct harms with different human consequences:

High FPR → silencing of legitimate voices from that community (over-censorship)
High FNR → failure to protect that community from actual attacks (under-protection)

The insight that makes your project non-trivial is that these two errors often trade off. Making a model more sensitive (to catch more hate) increases FPR. Making it more conservative (to reduce false alarms) increases FNR. Fixing both simultaneously requires more than just tuning a threshold.
The severity thresholds (Minimal/Moderate/Serious at 0.02/0.05) are operationalisations of this. The Jigsaw competition itself used similar thresholds to judge submitted models. Your Phase 2 audit produced a concrete picture: muslim was Serious on both FPR and FNR for the LR model, meaning the model both over-flags innocent Muslim-mentioning comments and misses actual anti-Muslim hate. That's the specific injury being addressed.

Phase 3 — Why DistilBERT? Why the Flat 3× Weight?
Why move from LR to BERT at all?
LR with TF-IDF treats every word independently. "I love black music" and "I hate black people" have roughly similar TF-IDF vectors — both contain the word "black." LR has no way to distinguish them without seeing enough training examples of each exact phrase.
BERT reads the whole sentence at once. Its attention mechanism allows it to relate "love" to "music" and "hate" to "people" within the same sentence. It understands context, not just word presence. For toxicity detection, this should matter enormously — most hate speech is contextual, not keyword-based.
Why DistilBERT specifically? BERT-base has 110 million parameters. Training it takes hours on a T4 GPU. DistilBERT is a compressed version — 40% smaller, 60% faster, retaining 97% of BERT's language understanding ability through a technique called knowledge distillation (a smaller "student" model learns to mimic a larger "teacher" model's outputs rather than training from raw data alone). For a 2-credit mini project with Colab T4 sessions, this is the practical choice.
The flat 3× weight rationale and its flaw:
The logic was: if the model is failing on identity-mentioning comments, make the model pay more attention to them by increasing the gradient signal from those samples. Standard CrossEntropyLoss averages the error across all samples equally. A sample with weight 3× contributes 3× as much to the gradient update.
The flaw, which Phase 4 revealed: this treats all identity-mentioning comments the same — whether they're toxic or clean. Upweighting a clean comment that mentions "muslim" with 3× weight means the model gets a 3× penalty when it classifies that comment correctly as toxic. But wait — the model might classify it incorrectly as toxic (FPR error), and when it does, it gets a 3× penalty for that error too. So the model is simultaneously being told: "pay more attention to muslim-mentioning comments" — but the label on many of those comments is "non-toxic." The model resolves this tension by becoming confused. It sees "muslim" + high weight + toxic label frequently enough (in the toxic subset) that the word "muslim" itself becomes more strongly weighted toward toxicity in its representations. The FPR gap explodes.
Why did no one catch this in Phase 3? Because Phase 3 had no control. Without a Vanilla BERT comparison, the worsened FPR gaps weren't visible — you just had BERT + weights producing numbers that looked reasonable in isolation.

Phase 4 — The Controlled Experiment
Why is this the most important phase scientifically?
Phase 4 is where the project stops being engineering and becomes research. The design — three models evaluated on identical test sets — is the minimal valid scientific comparison. Every variable is held constant except the weight scheme. Same architecture, same hyperparameters, same random seed, same balanced dataset, same test set.
The Vanilla BERT finding and what it means:
Vanilla BERT (no weights, pure fine-tuning on balanced data) produced dramatically worse FPR gaps than LR baseline — black went from 0.25 (LR) to 0.78 (Vanilla BERT). This is a striking finding in itself: a more powerful model, trained more carefully, with no identifiable mistakes, produced worse demographic fairness than a much simpler model.
Why? Because BERT's contextual understanding cuts both ways. LR learned "muslim mentions toxic content in training data" as a weak, averaged statistical signal. BERT learned the same correlation, but because it understands sentence structure, it learned it more precisely — it learned that "muslim" appearing in sentences with aggressive political language is a strong predictor of the toxic label in the training data. This is a more sophisticated representation of the same spurious correlation.
This finding resonates with what researchers call the "accuracy-fairness tradeoff" — improving a model's ability to fit the training data can worsen its demographic behaviour if the training data carries demographic bias in its annotations.
The Adaptive BERT vs Vanilla BERT comparison:
Adaptive BERT (weights derived from Phase 2 FPR gaps) improved slightly on Vanilla BERT for FPR in some groups (muslim: 0.44 → 0.41) but remained Serious everywhere. The FNR gaps were consistently good. This is the data that proves the Phase 5 motivation: proportional upweighting alone is insufficient because it doesn't distinguish between the two types of identity-mentioning comments.
Phase 5 — Three Simultaneous Fixes
Why three interventions together rather than one at a time?
Ideally, you'd test each intervention in isolation — one model per intervention, holding everything else constant, in a fully factorial design. But you have Colab T4 time constraints and a 2-credit deadline. The decision to combine all three reflects a practical choice that is disclosed and defensible: you're testing the upper bound of what this approach can achieve, not the isolated contribution of each component.
Asymmetric loss — the intuition:
The Phase 4 finding told you that the problem was specifically in how clean identity-mentioning comments are handled. So the fix needs to be targeted specifically there.
Think of it as three categories of comments:
A toxic comment mentioning an identity group ("All Muslims should be banned") — this is the exact harm we want the model to catch. Weight = 3.0×. Big penalty if you miss this.
A clean comment mentioning an identity group ("The Muslim community organised a food drive") — this is where the FPR errors come from. Weight = 0.4×. Small penalty if you flag this — the model should be cautious, not aggressive.
All other comments — Weight = 1.0×. Standard treatment.
The 0.4× is not arbitrary: it's the inverse complement of the 3.0×. The idea is that the net gradient signal on identity-mentioning comments should be roughly centred — the model learns both to catch real hate AND to tolerate innocent mentions, in roughly equal measure.
CDA — the intuition:
Imagine showing a child photographs to teach them about dangerous animals. If all your tiger photos are in jungles but all your cat photos are in houses, the child might learn "jungle = tiger" rather than "stripes + sharp teeth = tiger." You've taught them a location correlation rather than the actual visual features.
CDA does the equivalent of showing tigers in houses and cats in jungles. By generating "He was a great leader" alongside "She was a great leader" with the same label, the model cannot use gender as a predictor — the sentence structure and sentiment are identical, only the identity word differs. The spurious correlation between identity words and toxicity labels is directly disrupted.
In your implementation, you generated within-group swaps only (religion↔religion, gender↔gender, race↔race). You did not cross-substitute across categories, which would create nonsensical sentences ("the christian community was attacked by other christians" → swap to "the muslim community was attacked by other muslims" is valid; swapping "christian" to "male" would produce incoherent text).
The 1.35× augmentation ratio (3,827 new samples on top of 11,064 original) is modest. CDA's effectiveness scales with how thoroughly it covers the identity terms in the data — a richer substitution dictionary and more training data would amplify the effect.
HateBERT — the intuition:
DistilBERT was trained on Wikipedia articles and BookCorpus. These are grammatically clean, formally written, editorially reviewed texts. Nobody writing an encyclopedia article says "go back to your country, you [slur]." Nobody in a novel writes in the fragmented, abbreviated, aggressive register of Twitter comment sections.
HateBERT was trained on approximately 1.5 million posts from Reddit communities that were banned for hate speech violations. It has seen the actual linguistic patterns of online toxicity: the dog whistles, the plausible deniability phrasing, the coded language, the indirect attacks. Its internal representations of words like "invasion" (used metaphorically for immigration), "replacement" (white replacement conspiracy), or "grooming" (used in extremist anti-LGBTQ rhetoric) are tuned to their hateful rather than their neutral meanings.
This directly addresses the FNR problem. The comments your Phase 4 model missed with confidence 0.02–0.05 — things like "What an appropriate ban would be on Muslims" or "That's right, it takes a man to show women how to be a woman" — are exactly the kind of indirect, register-specific toxicity that DistilBERT has no pretraining exposure to. HateBERT has.
The LOAD REPORT warnings you saw in training are not errors. HateBERT was saved with old TensorFlow-era naming for LayerNorm parameters (gamma/beta) while current PyTorch uses (weight/bias). HuggingFace remaps these automatically. The model trained correctly — your epoch-level validation loss and F1 confirm this.
Cross-dataset evaluation — the intuition:
This is the test of whether you built something real or something that just memorised the Jigsaw annotation patterns.
The Jigsaw dataset was annotated by Civil Comments platform users — a specific population with specific biases, evaluating comments on specific types of news article comment sections. HateXplain was annotated by crowdworkers on Amazon Mechanical Turk, evaluating tweets and Gab posts, which have entirely different registers, community norms, and forms of toxicity.
If your model genuinely learned "what makes speech harmful," it should transfer reasonably to HateXplain. If it learned "what Civil Comments annotators call toxic," it will fail badly on HateXplain.
Your result: F1 dropped from 0.860 to 0.772 (8.8 percentage points) — moderate generalisation. The threshold analysis showing that optimal threshold shifts from 0.50 to 0.15 is particularly important: it means the model's internal confidence scores are miscalibrated for HateXplain's label distribution. The model is systematically too conservative on HateXplain — it produces low confidence scores on content that HateXplain annotators label as harmful. This is a calibration failure — the model's probability estimates don't match the new domain's label distribution — on top of a representation gap.

The Central Finding, Stated Precisely
Your project's core empirical contribution is this:
In toxicity classification with demographic fairness constraints, symmetric loss upweighting of identity-mentioning samples reduces FNR gaps (improves recall on targeted hate) but substantially amplifies FPR gaps (increases over-flagging of innocent identity-mentioning content). These two error types respond oppositely to the same training intervention, demonstrating that they require separate mechanisms: asymmetric loss weighting (penalising FP on clean identity content) for FPR correction, and domain-specific pretraining (HateBERT) for FNR correction. The combination, augmented with CDA, produces group-differential outcomes: religion-group bias (muslim, christian) responds well to joint intervention, while race-group bias (black) remains resistant, suggesting annotation-level rather than model-level causes for the latter.

Any More Questions?
A few questions worth thinking through before you write the report, because a reviewer might ask them:
Why 100k samples from 1.8M? Computational constraint. Worth stating explicitly. The subgroup sizes (muslim n=29, black n=39) are a direct consequence of this — on the full dataset, you'd have roughly 18× more subgroup samples and much more stable FPR/FNR estimates.
Why 50/50 class balance? Because the natural distribution (92/8) makes accuracy misleading and makes subgroup metrics unreliable. But this means your models were not trained on the real-world distribution, so deployment performance would differ. Worth one sentence in limitations.
Why FPR/FNR and not the Jigsaw competition metrics (subgroup AUC)? FPR/FNR are more interpretable and directly map to the two real-world harms (over-censorship, under-protection). Subgroup AUC is ranking-based and harder to explain to a non-technical audience. Your choice is defensible — just state it.
Why not test on the full Jigsaw test set? Because the test set doesn't have toxicity labels (it was the competition's held-out set). You used a train-test split from the training data, which is standard practice.
