Option A — stratum-level ∆D (aggregate first, then compare strata):
You compute one ∆D per stratum by averaging across all professions within it. So Healthcare stratum gets one number, Manual Labor gets one number, and you compare those. The claim you can make is: "disability conditioning produces stronger drift in manual labor than healthcare as a category." But you've lost within-stratum variance. If Construction Worker drifts heavily but Factory Worker doesn't, they cancel out and you miss it.
Option B — profession-level ∆D (individual first, then compare across strata):
You compute ∆D per profession, keep them as individual data points, and then use stratum membership as a grouping variable in your statistical test. Construction Worker, Electrician, Factory Worker each get their own ∆D. You then ask: do the ∆D values of Manual Labor professions as a group differ significantly from Healthcare professions as a group? The claim becomes: "professions in the manual labor stratum show systematically higher drift than those in healthcare." You preserve within-stratum variance and your test has more statistical power because you have more data points.
Option B is the correct approach, and here's the precise reason: your hypothesis is about whether socioeconomic stratum predicts drift magnitude. To test that, stratum is your independent variable and ∆D is your dependent variable. You need ∆D at the observation level — one per profession — not pre-aggregated. Pre-aggregating before testing is a form of pseudoreplication; you're artificially reducing your degrees of freedom and potentially smoothing out exactly the signal you're trying to detect.
The Kruskal-Wallis test I put in the pipeline diagram operates on individual ∆D values grouped by stratum — that's why Option B is what that test expects. If you aggregated to one value per stratum, you'd have maybe 8 data points total across strata, which isn't enough for any meaningful non-parametric test.
One further nuance worth flagging: within Option B, you should also report which specific professions drive the stratum effect. If Kruskal-Wallis tells you strata differ significantly, follow it with a Dunn post-hoc test to identify which stratum pairs are driving the difference, then point at the individual profession ∆D values to explain why.
